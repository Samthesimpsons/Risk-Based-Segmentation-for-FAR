\subsection{Baseline Audit}
\label{sec:results:baseline}
The two FAR-Trans baselines sit at opposite corners of the return-accuracy tradeoff, and neither balances the three axes (Table~\ref{tab:aggregate}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Random Forest} wins return ($+1.4$\%/mo) but ranks near random (nDCG@10 of $0.019$), so its balance collapses to $0.054$.
    \item \textbf{LightGCN} wins ranking accuracy (nDCG@10 of $0.330$, PC@10 of $0.794$) but earns a slightly negative ROI@10, for a balance of $0.428$.
    \item Aggregate PC@10 looks adequate for both ($1.17\times$ lift for LightGCN, $1.06\times$ for Random Forest), but this single number averages over bands whose random baselines $\pi(b)$ span $0.35$ to $0.78$ and so hides which bands are actually served.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:perband} exposes that gap. Per-band lift rises monotonically with the declared band for both baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Random Forest:} $0.52 \to 0.70 \to 1.03 \to 1.88$ from Conservative to Aggressive.
    \item \textbf{LightGCN:} $0.75 \to 1.13 \to 1.17 \to 1.35$ over the same bands.
    \item Both skew towards higher-risk assets and under-serve the Conservative band below the random baseline, and Random Forest also under-serves Income.
    \item Aggressive's high lift is partly mechanical, since $\pi(\text{Aggressive}) = 0.35$ is the lowest bar to clear.
\end{itemize}

Neither baseline carries band labels or a band-aware objective, so per-band coherence is likely a passive byproduct of training rather than a controlled output. This is the deficit a band-aware approach must close.

\subsection{Aggregate Results of Risk-Based Segmentation}
\label{sec:results:aggregate}
Reading the ablation down Table~\ref{tab:aggregate} separates a cheap architectural step from the two levers:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Segmentation alone} lifts PC@10 to $0.821$ at roughly flat ranking (nDCG@10 of $0.332$) and a small ROI cost, for a balance of $0.411$, marginally below the baseline.
    \item \textbf{Model lever (margin loss)} pushes PC@10 to $0.918$ but spends accuracy doing so, dropping nDCG@10 to $0.309$, for a balance of $0.407$ that also sits below the baseline.
    \item \textbf{Data lever (regrouping)} lifts PC@10 to $0.949$ while \emph{also} raising nDCG@10 to $0.352$ and leaving ROI@10 essentially unchanged, for a balance of $0.452$, the highest of any configuration and the only one above the LightGCN baseline.
    \item \textbf{Combined} reaches the highest raw PC@10 ($0.982$) but its balance ($0.444$) falls back below regrouping alone, because the margin loss spends accuracy even on the corrected bands.
\end{itemize}
Of the two exclusive levers, only regrouping raises the harmonic-mean balance, because it corrects the noisy declared labels at their source rather than fighting them during training. The proof is in nDCG@10 and ROI@10, neither of which uses the risk band: both still rise under regrouping, which is only possible if the declared bands had been adding noise to training. The declared bands, not the model, were holding performance back. The recommended operating point is therefore the data lever alone, since once the bands are corrected the margin loss is no longer necessary and even lowers the balance from $0.452$ to $0.444$.

\subsection{Per-Band Suitability}
\label{sec:results:perband}
The per-band lifts in Table~\ref{tab:perband} trace where each lever spends its effort:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Margin loss} is largely spent rescuing the two extremes the baselines failed, dragging Conservative from $0.75$ past the random baseline to $1.19$ and Aggressive from $1.35$ to $1.97$.
    \item \textbf{Segmentation alone} barely moves Conservative ($0.75 \to 0.91$, still below random) and even lowers Aggressive to $1.17$, because partitioning by declared band still trains each sub-model on the same discordant purchases: the underlying labels stay uncorrected, so coherence cannot rise much.
    \item \textbf{Regrouping} clears the random baseline in every band ($1.21$, $1.25$, $1.24$, $2.24$).
    \item \textbf{Combined} reaches the highest per-band lifts of all ($1.25$, $1.26$, $1.30$, $2.68$).
\end{itemize}
The Conservative deficit that both baselines exhibited is thus closed by either lever, but only regrouping closes it without the accuracy penalty seen in the aggregate.

\subsection{Consistency Across Splits}
\label{sec:results:splits}
The aggregate gains are not artefacts of averaging over a few strong splits. Figure~\ref{fig:paired-deltas} gives the per-split deltas against the baseline and Figure~\ref{fig:win-rates} the win rate, the fraction of splits on which a configuration beats the baseline:
\begin{itemize}
    \item PC@10 is positive on every one of the 69 splits for all three configurations.
    \item The balance delta is positive only for regrouping ($+0.019$ on average) and negative for both segmentation alone and the margin loss.
    \item Regrouping wins the balance on $71\%$ of splits and nDCG@10 on all of them.
    \item The margin loss wins the balance on only $26\%$ of splits and nDCG@10 on a mere $7\%$, so its accuracy cost is a uniform downward shift rather than a handful of bad splits.
\end{itemize}
The individual splits therefore tell the same story as the averages: regrouping beats the baseline, and the margin loss falls short of it.
